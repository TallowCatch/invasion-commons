\begin{abstract}
Advanced AI systems are increasingly expected to operate in plural settings rather than as a single monolithic agent. That shift changes the alignment question from the behavior of one overseen system to the robustness of institutions that govern many interacting systems under strategic pressure. This working draft recenters the sequential-commons benchmark around that question. Fishery Commons is retained as a precursor signal-design study showing that central interventions can be ranked under adversarial strategy injection, with adaptive quotas emerging as the strongest overall top-down signal in the medium tier. Harvest Commons becomes the main architecture study: current high-power evidence already shows that hybrid governance improves local ecological control relative to top-down-only control under a stronger search-based invader, with a measurable welfare trade-off. The next experimental objective is to extend that result into a fuller institutional comparison by restoring the full architecture spectrum, making the attacker ladder explicit, and identifying who bears the welfare cost of robust governance.
\end{abstract}
