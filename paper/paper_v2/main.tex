\begin{abstract}
We study governance under adversarial strategy injection in sequential commons through two linked studies. Study~1 uses a minimal fishery commons to compare central governance signals under repeated strategy turnover and held-out regime shifts. A matched mutation-versus-live-LLM baseline shows that monitoring with sanctions reduces held-out collapse relative to no governance for both injector types, and a broader top-down sweep over partner mixes and pressure levels identifies adaptive quotas as the strongest overall signal, with temporary closures remaining competitive in hostile partner mixes. Study~2 introduces Harvest Commons to compare governance architectures rather than only signal choices. Across a tiered matrix over social compositions, top-down-only, bottom-up-only, and hybrid architectures, the hybrid condition wins most cells on patch health and local overharvest control, though it often pays a welfare cost in harsher adversarial settings. The combined result is a two-level evaluation protocol: first identify effective central governance signals in a controlled commons, then test how broader governance architectures behave in a second substrate.
\end{abstract}
