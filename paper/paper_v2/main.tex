\begin{abstract}
We study governance under adversarial strategy injection in sequential commons through two linked studies. The first study uses a minimal Fishery Commons environment to compare central governance signals under repeated strategy turnover and held-out regime shifts. A matched mutation-versus-live-LLM baseline shows that monitoring with sanctions reduces held-out collapse relative to no governance for both injector types. A broader top-down sweep over partner mixes and pressure levels then identifies adaptive quotas as the strongest overall signal, and a learned-policy self-play validation in the medium tier recovers the same ordering: adaptive quotas outperform no governance on held-out collapse, stock, and welfare while also exceeding the other governed conditions. The second study extends the same invasion protocol to Harvest Commons and shifts the governance question from signal design to architecture design, with the manuscript's main comparison restricted to top-down-only versus hybrid control. In the high-power search-over-mutations matrix, hybrid ranks first in all eight decision cells against top-down-only control under a failure-then-patch-health-then-welfare rule, improving patch health and usually reducing neighborhood overharvest, with a moderate average welfare cost. A narrower medium-tier PPO validation yields concordant evidence: hybrid self-play improves patch health and lowers local aggression and neighborhood overharvest relative to top-down-only control, with only a small welfare cost. Together, the studies provide a two-level evaluation protocol that first identifies effective central governance signals in a controlled commons and then tests a narrower governance-architecture comparison in a second substrate under the same turnover pressure.
\end{abstract}
