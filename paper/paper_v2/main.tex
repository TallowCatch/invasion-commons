\begin{abstract}
We study governance under adversarial strategy injection in sequential commons through two linked studies. The first study uses a minimal Fishery Commons setting to compare central governance signals under repeated strategy turnover and held-out regime shifts. A matched mutation-versus-live-LLM baseline shows that monitoring with sanctions reduces held-out collapse relative to no governance for both injector types, and a broader top-down sweep over partner mixes and pressure levels identifies adaptive quotas as the strongest overall signal, with temporary closures remaining competitive in hostile partner mixes. The second study upgrades Harvest Commons to the same invasion logic and compares governance architectures rather than only signal choices. In a high-power Harvest invasion matrix over tiers, partner mixes, injector families, and adversarial pressures, the hybrid condition wins 14 of 16 decision cells against top-down-only control, improving patch health and neighborhood overharvest with only a small average welfare cost. The combined result is a two-level evaluation protocol: first identify effective central governance signals in a controlled commons, then test how broader governance architectures behave in a second substrate under the same turnover pressure.
\end{abstract}
