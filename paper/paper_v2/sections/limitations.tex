\section{Limitations}

The two studies answer adjacent questions, not identical ones. The first study is a controlled signal-ranking study in Fishery Commons, while the second study is a governance-architecture extension in Harvest Commons. The second study now shares the same invasion loop and replacement logic as the first, which fixes the earlier asymmetry, but it still should not be read as a strict replication in another substrate because the governance question itself is broader.

The LLM comparison currently appears only in the Fishery Commons study. This is enough to show that minimal central governance survives both mutation and live JSON-based strategy generation, but it does not yet establish the same result for Harvest Commons. Harvest Commons now uses the same turnover logic and non-LLM injector families as Fishery Commons, but live language-model-based Harvest injection is still deferred.

Hard settings remain useful mainly as stress evidence. In fishery, once collapse saturates at 1.0 and survival at 0.0 across conditions, governance separation becomes non-identifiable under floor effects. In Harvest Commons, the harder tiers remain informative, but garden failure itself does not separate conditions in the high-power rows; the real signal comes from patch health, welfare, and local behavioral metrics.

Finally, both substrates remain intentionally stylized. That is a strength for identification, but it limits external realism. Market feedbacks, richer heterogeneity, richer language interaction, and explicit institutional bargaining are natural next additions once the current governance comparisons are frozen.
