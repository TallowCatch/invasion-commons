\section{Discussion}

The paper now supports two related claims rather than one narrow claim. First, central governance signals can be ranked under adversarial strategy injection in a minimal sequential commons. The first, Fishery Commons study shows that monitoring with sanctions is already enough to improve held-out collapse under both mutation and live LLM injection, which is the minimal robustness result. The broader signal sweep then shows that stronger top-down interventions are not interchangeable: adaptive quotas are the best overall policy in medium-tier fishery runs, while temporary closures remain a credible fallback under adversarial-heavy partner mixes. This is more useful than simply saying ``governance helps,'' because it distinguishes light-touch enforcement from stronger adaptive intervention.

Second, governance should not be treated only as a central toggle. The second, Harvest Commons study asks what happens when the governance question is widened from signal design to architecture design. The answer is that hybrid governance can improve local ecological control beyond top-down-only control, but not for free. The earlier Harvest pilot and lighter invasion pass made that direction plausible. The stronger search-over-mutations stress test then makes it credible. In the high-power Harvest matrix, hybrid wins all 8 decision cells and improves patch health in every tier-by-partner block, but several rows now show a clearer welfare cost than before. This is exactly the kind of trade-off that disappears if evaluation only reports a single collapse number. The Harvest results therefore justify the move from a single global stock to a second substrate with local interactions and communication.

That broader framing is why the two studies belong in one paper. The Fishery Commons study isolates which central signals work in a highly controlled environment. The Harvest Commons study then asks whether governance remains purely central once local coordination, communication, and targeted intervention are allowed to matter. Commons experiments have long suggested that communication and collective choice can improve outcomes even without a single command-and-control institution \citep{ostrom1992covenants,walker2000collective}. The present results update that intuition for adversarially changing agent populations: once strategy turnover is explicit, governance must be evaluated both as a set of central signals and as a broader architecture for decentralized systems.

This also clarifies why people should care beyond fisheries or orchards. The contribution is not fish-specific. It is about institution design for self-interested multi-agent systems under uncertainty and turnover. The same protocol can be read as a testbed for shared compute budgets, energy systems, congestion control, spectrum allocation, or decentralized markets, where new behaviors keep entering and governance must balance resilience against performance. Recent generative-society work argues for realistic evaluation under diverse partners and conditions \citep{piatti2024cooperate,govsim_repo,mercer2023willowbrook}; the present paper adds a more specific lesson that governance should be tested both at the level of signal choice and at the level of governance architecture, and that stronger invaders can tighten but need not overturn those conclusions.
