\section{Discussion}

The hard tier is treated as appendix-only evidence because it enters a metric-saturation regime where governance separation is no longer identifiable. In the hard-tier smoke run, all governance conditions converge to held-out collapse equal to 1.0, per-regime survival equal to 0.0, and time-to-collapse equal to 0.0. This is a collapse-floor effect: once the system reliably collapses immediately under every condition, collapse-based metrics have no remaining dynamic range for mechanism comparison. In this setting, the hard tier is still useful as a stress confirmation that the environment can reach failure extremes, but it cannot support causal ranking of governance interventions.

The environment is intentionally minimal, and this is a design choice for identification rather than a claim of full ecological realism. By restricting dynamics to a controlled renewable-stock game, the mapping from governance signals to invasion outcomes remains interpretable, and the formal quantities in Section~\\ref{sec:theory} can be estimated without confounding from additional modules such as market-price feedback, spatial migration, or multi-resource coupling. The trade-off is external validity: larger-world policy conclusions require re-testing the same invasion-stability operator after adding complexity.

The broader implication for generative societies is that the contribution is not fishery-specific, but protocol-specific. Recent LLM-agent society studies show emergence and fragility in social outcomes \citep{piatti2024cooperate,govsim_repo}, while adversarial generation work emphasizes persistent pressure and strategy turnover \citep{sakana2026drq}. The present results suggest a transferable governance insight: under repeated strategy injection, lightweight institutional signals that increase observability and accountability can reduce exploitative pressure and preserve shared-system health in held-out regimes. This supports using invasion-stability evaluation as a bridge from controlled commons simulations to more complex generative-society governance settings \citep{mercer2023willowbrook}.
