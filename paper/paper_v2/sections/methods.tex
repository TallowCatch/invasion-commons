\section{Methods}

Both studies use the same core invasion protocol. A population of strategies is evaluated over generations, weaker strategies are removed, and new strategies are injected through a specified generator. Selection is performed in train conditions and reported robustness is measured in held-out test conditions. This separates adaptation pressure from generalization assessment and makes governance comparisons about out-of-distribution stability rather than only in-sample performance.

\subsection{Study 1: Fishery Commons and central governance signals}

The first study uses a minimal renewable fishery commons with a shared stock, per-agent harvest actions, regeneration, and collapse dynamics. Agents follow interpretable threshold strategies, and governance changes extraction pressure directly through monitoring, quota clipping, sanctions, adaptive quotas, or temporary closures. The environment logs requested harvest, realized harvest, quota-clipped harvest, repeat-offender rate, closure activity, and stock-recovery lag so that governance can be analyzed mechanistically rather than only through final collapse outcomes.

Study~1 has two layers. The first is a matched baseline that compares \texttt{none}, \texttt{monitoring}, and \texttt{monitoring\_sanctions} across two injector types: mutation and live JSON-based LLM injection via Ollama using \texttt{qwen2.5:3b-instruct}. This baseline is run on the easy\_v1 and medium\_v1 tiers. The second layer is a broader top-down sweep, restricted to medium\_v1, that compares \texttt{none}, \texttt{monitoring\_sanctions}, \texttt{adaptive\_quota}, and \texttt{temporary\_closure} under three non-LLM injector families: mutation, an adversarial heuristic injector, and search over mutations. The sweep also varies partner composition (\texttt{balanced} and \texttt{adversarial\_heavy}) and adversarial pressure (0.3 and 0.5). This second layer is what identifies which central governance signals remain effective once the setting becomes more hostile.

\subsection{Study 2: Harvest Commons and governance architecture}

The second study introduces Harvest Commons, a second renewable commons substrate built around local patches rather than a single global stock. Agents remain self-interested, but the substrate adds local communication, limited credit transfers, and targeted intervention through a government agent. The purpose is not to repeat the same top-down question in another skin. The purpose is to ask what changes when governance is treated as an architecture choice.

Study~2 compares four governance conditions: \texttt{none}, \texttt{top\_down\_only}, \texttt{bottom\_up\_only}, and \texttt{hybrid}. Top-down-only uses targeted caps from the government agent. Bottom-up-only uses local communication and side-payments without central intervention. Hybrid combines the two. The matrix spans three tier presets (\texttt{easy\_h1}, \texttt{medium\_h1}, and \texttt{hard\_h1}) and three social mixes (\texttt{cooperative\_heavy}, \texttt{mixed\_pressure}, and \texttt{adversarial\_heavy}) with 12 runs per cell. In addition to patch health and welfare, the study reports local mechanism metrics including local aggression and neighborhood overharvest. These are needed because a governance architecture can change \emph{where} exploitation happens, not only the global average outcome.

\subsection{Statistical reporting}

The primary Study~1 matched-baseline outcome is held-out collapse reduction, reported as the difference between no governance and monitoring with sanctions. Supporting metrics are held-out stock, time-to-collapse, and per-regime survival. Confidence intervals for the matched baseline are run-level bootstrap intervals with 5{,}000 resamples. The broader Study~1 signal sweep uses the same run summaries but is interpreted as a ranking study: adaptive quota, temporary closure, and monitoring with sanctions are compared on collapse first and welfare second.

The primary Study~2 comparison is the paired difference \(\texttt{hybrid} - \texttt{top\_down\_only}\) at matched run identifiers within each tier and social-mix cell. This comparison isolates whether local communication and credit-sharing add anything once central intervention is already present. Confidence intervals are again estimated by bootstrap over paired deltas.

Formal invasion dynamics, the definition of collapse robustness, and the saturation identifiability condition are given in Section~\ref{sec:theory}. In the empirical pipeline, these quantities are estimated from run-level summaries and frozen result artifacts to avoid post-hoc threshold drift.
