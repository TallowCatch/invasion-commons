\section{Methods}

Both studies use the same core invasion protocol. A population of strategies is evaluated over generations, weaker strategies are removed, and new strategies are injected through a specified generator. Selection is performed in train conditions and reported robustness is measured in held-out test conditions. This separates adaptation pressure from generalization assessment and makes governance comparisons about out-of-distribution stability rather than only in-sample performance.

\subsection{First study: Fishery Commons and central governance signals}

The first study uses a minimal renewable fishery commons with a shared stock, per-agent harvest actions, regeneration, and collapse dynamics. Agents follow interpretable threshold strategies, and governance changes extraction pressure directly through monitoring, quota clipping, sanctions, adaptive quotas, or temporary closures. The environment logs requested harvest, realized harvest, quota-clipped harvest, repeat-offender rate, closure activity, and stock-recovery lag so that governance can be analyzed mechanistically rather than only through final collapse outcomes.

The Fishery Commons study has two layers. The first is a matched baseline that compares no governance, monitoring alone, and monitoring with sanctions across two injector types: mutation and live JSON-based LLM injection through Ollama with the `qwen2.5:3b-instruct` model. This baseline is run on the easy and medium fishery tiers. The second layer is a broader top-down sweep, restricted to the medium tier, that compares no governance, monitoring with sanctions, adaptive quotas, and temporary closures under three non-LLM injector families: mutation, an adversarial heuristic injector, and search over mutations. The sweep also varies partner composition between balanced and adversarial-heavy populations, and it varies adversarial pressure between 0.3 and 0.5. This second layer is what identifies which central governance signals remain effective once the setting becomes more hostile.

\subsection{Second study: Harvest Commons and governance architecture}

The second study introduces Harvest Commons, a second renewable commons substrate built around local patches rather than a single global stock. It now uses the same invasion logic as the first study rather than only a fixed-composition matrix. A population of six parameterized Harvest strategies is evaluated, ranked, and partially replaced over generations. The evolving strategies control local harvest fractions, restraint signals, credit requests, credit offers, reciprocity weights, and cap-compliance margins. Communication and side-payments remain condition-controlled by the environment, so the same evolving strategy family can be evaluated under different governance architectures.

The Harvest Commons study compares four governance conditions: none, top-down only, bottom-up only, and hybrid. Top-down only uses a fixed government agent that observes patch stress and local aggressive neighborhoods, then applies targeted caps. Bottom-up only enables local communication and side-payments without central intervention. Hybrid combines the fixed government agent with local communication and credit transfers. The main invasion study uses three non-LLM injectors, namely random injection, mutation, and an adversarial heuristic injector, together with held-out regime packs for each tier. The earlier fixed-composition Harvest matrix is retained only as a pilot precursor and is not the main evidence for the second study.

The Stage B Harvest invasion pilot spans three tier presets, easy, medium, and hard, together with three partner mixes, cooperative-heavy, balanced, and adversarial-heavy. It covers all four governance conditions, all three injector families, and adversarial pressure 0.3. The decision-critical Stage C matrix then concentrates on the medium and hard tiers, the balanced and adversarial-heavy partner mixes, top-down only versus hybrid governance, and the two stronger non-LLM injectors, mutation and adversarial heuristic, at pressures 0.3 and 0.5. Each of those cells is run for 15 generations with 32 train and 32 test seeds per generation. In addition to patch health and welfare, the Harvest Commons study reports local mechanism metrics including local aggression, neighborhood overharvest, capped-action rate, targeted-agent fraction, and prevented harvest. These are needed because a governance architecture can change \emph{where} exploitation happens, not only the global average outcome.

\subsection{Statistical reporting}

The primary matched-baseline outcome in the Fishery Commons study is held-out collapse reduction, reported as the difference between no governance and monitoring with sanctions. Supporting metrics are held-out stock, time-to-collapse, and per-regime survival. Confidence intervals for the matched baseline are run-level bootstrap intervals with 5{,}000 resamples. The broader Fishery Commons sweep uses the same run summaries but is interpreted as a ranking study: adaptive quota, temporary closure, and monitoring with sanctions are compared on collapse first and welfare second.

The primary comparison in the Harvest Commons study is the paired difference between hybrid and top-down-only governance at matched run identifiers within each tier, partner mix, injector, and adversarial-pressure cell. This comparison isolates whether local communication and credit sharing add anything once central intervention is already present. Confidence intervals are again estimated by bootstrap over paired deltas. In the high-power Stage C matrix, garden failure is never the discriminating outcome, so the main Harvest Commons evidence is carried by patch health, welfare, local aggression, and neighborhood overharvest.

Formal invasion dynamics, the definition of collapse robustness, and the saturation identifiability condition are given in Section~\ref{sec:theory}. In the empirical pipeline, these quantities are estimated from run-level summaries and frozen result artifacts to avoid post-hoc threshold drift.
