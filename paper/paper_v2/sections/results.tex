\section{Results}

\subsection{Study 1: central governance signals in Fishery Commons}

The matched Fishery Commons baseline shows that even a minimal central intervention survives repeated injection pressure. Under five paired high-power runs in medium\_v1, monitoring with sanctions reduces held-out collapse relative to no governance for both injector types. For mutation, collapse reduction is \(0.1099\) with 95\% CI \([0.0310, 0.1896]\). For live JSON-based LLM injection, collapse reduction is \(0.0835\) with 95\% CI \([0.0154, 0.1509]\). Mean held-out stock also rises substantially, by \(23.35\) for mutation and \(22.40\) for live LLM injection. These matched results establish that the invasion-stability protocol is not an artifact of one injector family and that a lightweight central signal already improves robustness.

The broader top-down sweep in medium\_v1 sharpens the central-governance question. Across balanced and adversarial-heavy partner mixes, pressure levels 0.3 and 0.5, and three non-LLM injector families, \texttt{adaptive\_quota} is the most frequent winner in every injector block. It wins all four scenarios for mutation, all four for the adversarial heuristic injector, and all four for search over mutations. This happens because \texttt{adaptive\_quota} and \texttt{temporary\_closure} tie on collapse reduction in most cells, but adaptive quotas usually retain more welfare outside the most hostile partner mixes.

The nuance is important. In balanced partner mixes, adaptive quotas dominate closures on welfare by large margins: \(4792.55\) for mutation, \(5202.70\) for the adversarial heuristic injector, and \(5367.27\) for search over mutations at pressure 0.3. In adversarial-heavy partner mixes, that advantage shrinks or reverses: the same adaptive-minus-closure welfare difference is \(-215.62\), \(-779.48\), and \(-71.33\) at pressure 0.3 for the three injectors. So the correct interpretation is not that temporary closures fail. It is that adaptive quotas are the strongest overall signal, while closures remain a serious hostile-population fallback when ecological protection is prioritized over welfare retention.

Mechanism logging explains why monitoring with sanctions is weaker than the more forceful top-down alternatives. Mean quota-clipped harvest under monitoring with sanctions is \(8.53\) for mutation, \(18.32\) for the adversarial heuristic injector, and \(9.61\) for search over mutations, while mean repeat-offender rates remain \(0.3032\), \(0.5568\), and \(0.3458\). Monitoring with sanctions therefore reduces harm without fully suppressing aggressive extraction, whereas adaptive quotas and temporary closures push collapse to zero in nearly every Study~1b cell.

\subsection{Study 2: governance architecture in Harvest Commons}

Study~2 shifts from signal design to governance architecture. Across the full Harvest Commons matrix of three tiers, three social mixes, four governance conditions, and 12 runs per cell, \texttt{hybrid} is the best condition in \(7/9\) tier-by-mix cells. \texttt{top\_down\_only} wins one cell and \texttt{bottom\_up\_only} wins one cell. Averaged over the full matrix, the paired difference \(\texttt{hybrid} - \texttt{top\_down\_only}\) is \(+0.1954\) for patch health and \(-0.2431\) for neighborhood overharvest, but \(-0.3748\) for welfare. The high-level pattern is therefore consistent: hybrid governance usually controls local ecological damage better than central control alone, but often does so by accepting some welfare cost.

The paired bootstrap intervals show where that trade-off is strongest. In \texttt{medium\_h1} with \texttt{adversarial\_heavy} social mix, the patch-health delta is \(+0.0460\) with 95\% CI \([0.0250, 0.0650]\), while the welfare delta is \(-0.7439\) with 95\% CI \([-0.9683, -0.5662]\). In \texttt{hard\_h1} with \texttt{adversarial\_heavy}, the corresponding deltas are \(+0.0677\) with 95\% CI \([0.0325, 0.1025]\) and \(-0.3722\) with 95\% CI \([-0.7746, -0.0330]\). By contrast, \texttt{hard\_h1} with \texttt{mixed\_pressure} is closer to a balanced trade-off: patch health improves by \(+0.0552\) with 95\% CI \([0.0070, 0.1016]\), while the welfare interval \([{-}0.0121, 0.3617]\) overlaps zero. This is the key Study~2 result: hybrid governance is not universally better, but it is consistently better on local ecological control and only sometimes better on welfare.

Together, the two studies give a layered result. In the controlled fishery setting, central governance signals can be ranked and adaptive quotas emerge as the strongest overall top-down signal under invasion pressure. In the second substrate, once governance is allowed to become architectural rather than only signal-level, hybrid governance often produces the best local ecological control, but with a welfare trade-off that depends on social composition and task difficulty.
