\section{Results}

\subsection{First study: central governance signals in Fishery Commons}

The matched Fishery Commons baseline shows that even a minimal central intervention survives repeated injection pressure. Under five paired high-power runs in the medium tier, monitoring with sanctions reduces held-out collapse relative to no governance for both injector types. For mutation, collapse reduction is \(0.1099\) with 95\% confidence interval \([0.0310, 0.1896]\). For live JSON-based LLM injection, collapse reduction is \(0.0835\) with 95\% confidence interval \([0.0154, 0.1509]\). Mean held-out stock also rises substantially, by \(23.35\) for mutation and \(22.40\) for live LLM injection. These matched results establish that the invasion-stability protocol is not an artifact of one injector family and that a lightweight central signal already improves robustness.

The broader top-down sweep in the medium fishery tier sharpens the central-governance question. Across balanced and adversarial-heavy partner mixes, pressure levels 0.3 and 0.5, and three non-LLM injector families, adaptive quotas are the most frequent winner in every injector block. They win all four scenarios for mutation, all four for the adversarial heuristic injector, and all four for search over mutations. This happens because adaptive quotas and temporary closures tie on collapse reduction in most cells, but adaptive quotas usually retain more welfare outside the most hostile partner mixes.

The nuance matters. In balanced partner mixes, adaptive quotas dominate closures on welfare by large margins: \(4792.55\) for mutation, \(5202.70\) for the adversarial heuristic injector, and \(5367.27\) for search over mutations at pressure 0.3. In adversarial-heavy partner mixes, that advantage shrinks or reverses: the same adaptive-minus-closure welfare difference is \(-215.62\), \(-779.48\), and \(-71.33\) at pressure 0.3 for the three injectors. So the correct interpretation is not that temporary closures fail. It is that adaptive quotas are the strongest overall signal, while closures remain a serious fallback when ecological protection is prioritized over welfare retention.

Mechanism logging explains why monitoring with sanctions is weaker than the more forceful top-down alternatives. Mean quota-clipped harvest under monitoring with sanctions is \(8.53\) for mutation, \(18.32\) for the adversarial heuristic injector, and \(9.61\) for search over mutations, while mean repeat-offender rates remain \(0.3032\), \(0.5568\), and \(0.3458\). Monitoring with sanctions therefore reduces harm without fully suppressing aggressive extraction, whereas adaptive quotas and temporary closures push collapse to zero in nearly every cell of the first Fishery Commons study.

\subsection{Second study: governance architecture in Harvest Commons}

The second study now uses the same invasion logic as the first, rather than only a fixed-composition comparison. The Stage B pilot sweep established that this architecture question was worth pursuing: under repeated replacement and non-LLM injectors, hybrid governance won 18 of 27 pilot cells and top-down-only control won the remaining 9. The main Harvest Commons evidence comes from the Stage C high-power matrix, which restricts attention to the decision-critical rows in the medium and hard tiers, the balanced and adversarial-heavy partner mixes, mutation and adversarial heuristic injection, and pressures 0.3 and 0.5.

In that high-power matrix, hybrid governance wins 14 of 16 cells. The only rows where top-down-only control still ranks first are the two medium-tier, balanced, adversarial-heuristic cells, where hybrid gains welfare but gives up some patch health. This is an important correction to the earlier fixed-composition narrative. Once Harvest Commons uses the same turnover logic as Fishery Commons, the main contrast is no longer ``can hybrid ever differ from top-down-only?'' It is ``where does hybrid buy ecological control, and when is the welfare price small enough to accept?''

The aggregated Stage C view makes that trade-off readable. In the hard tier with an adversarial-heavy population, the paired patch-health delta is \(+0.0339\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(0.0252\) to \(0.0447\), while the welfare delta is \(-0.0830\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(-0.1217\) to \(-0.0476\). In the hard tier with a balanced population, hybrid governance still improves patch health by \(+0.0306\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(0.0095\) to \(0.0523\), but the welfare interval from \(-0.1197\) to \(0.0016\) nearly touches zero.

In the medium tier with an adversarial-heavy population, the ecological gain is strongest: patch health rises by \(+0.0466\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(0.0208\) to \(0.0823\), though welfare again overlaps a modest cost. By contrast, the medium-tier balanced block is the one region where the trade-off flips direction. There, patch health falls by \(-0.0199\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(-0.0467\) to \(0.0069\), while welfare rises by \(+0.0936\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(0.0086\) to \(0.1834\). The Harvest Commons study therefore does not claim that hybrid governance dominates everywhere. It claims something more precise: once local strategic pressure becomes difficult enough, hybrid governance consistently improves ecological control relative to central intervention alone, and its welfare cost is often small compared with the ecological benefit.

These mechanism metrics explain why the failure metric is uninformative in the Harvest Commons study. Across all 16 high-power rows, the paired garden-failure delta is exactly zero, so governance separation happens before outright garden failure. Hybrid governance matters because it shifts local behavior: averaged over the full Stage C matrix, local aggression falls by \(0.0034\) and neighborhood overharvest falls by \(0.0352\) relative to top-down-only control. The stronger result is therefore behavioral and ecological rather than catastrophic.

Together, the two studies now give a layered invasion result. In the controlled fishery setting, central governance signals can be ranked and adaptive quotas emerge as the strongest overall top-down signal under invasion pressure. In the second substrate, once governance is allowed to become architectural rather than only signal-level, hybrid governance usually produces better local ecological control than top-down-only control, though the welfare effect depends on both social composition and task difficulty.
