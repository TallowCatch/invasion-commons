\section{Results}

\subsection{First study: central governance signals in Fishery Commons}

The matched Fishery Commons baseline shows that even a minimal central intervention survives repeated injection pressure. Under five paired high-power runs in the medium tier, monitoring with sanctions reduces held-out collapse relative to no governance for both injector types. For mutation, collapse reduction is \(0.1099\) with 95\% confidence interval \([0.0310, 0.1896]\). For live JSON-based LLM injection, collapse reduction is \(0.0835\) with 95\% confidence interval \([0.0154, 0.1509]\). Mean held-out stock also rises substantially, by \(23.35\) for mutation and \(22.40\) for live LLM injection. These matched results establish that the invasion-stability protocol is not an artifact of one injector family and that a lightweight central signal already improves robustness.

The broader top-down sweep in the medium fishery tier sharpens the central-governance question. Across balanced and adversarial-heavy partner mixes, pressure levels 0.3 and 0.5, and three non-LLM injector families, adaptive quotas are the most frequent winner in every injector block. They win all four scenarios for mutation, all four for the adversarial heuristic injector, and all four for search over mutations. This pattern arises because adaptive quotas and temporary closures tie on collapse reduction in most cells, but adaptive quotas usually retain more welfare outside the most hostile partner mixes.

The welfare comparison is heterogeneous across partner mixes. In balanced partner mixes, adaptive quotas dominate closures on welfare by large margins: \(4792.55\) for mutation, \(5202.70\) for the adversarial heuristic injector, and \(5367.27\) for search over mutations at pressure 0.3. In adversarial-heavy partner mixes, that advantage shrinks or reverses: the same adaptive-minus-closure welfare difference is \(-215.62\), \(-779.48\), and \(-71.33\) at pressure 0.3 for the three injectors. These results indicate that adaptive quotas are the strongest overall signal, while closures remain a credible fallback when ecological protection is prioritized over welfare retention.

Mechanism logging explains why monitoring with sanctions is weaker than the more forceful top-down alternatives. Mean quota-clipped harvest under monitoring with sanctions is \(8.53\) for mutation, \(18.32\) for the adversarial heuristic injector, and \(9.61\) for search over mutations, while mean repeat-offender rates remain \(0.3032\), \(0.5568\), and \(0.3458\). Monitoring with sanctions therefore reduces harm without fully suppressing aggressive extraction, whereas adaptive quotas and temporary closures push collapse to zero in nearly every cell of the first Fishery Commons study.

A learned-policy validation reaches the same conclusion. Under five PPO self-play training runs on the held-out `medium\_v1` pack, adaptive quotas reduce mean collapse from \(0.8141\) under no governance to \(0.0\), raise mean stock from \(97.56\) to \(186.05\), and raise mean welfare from \(4770.20\) to \(9902.35\). A matched governance follow-up under the same PPO protocol also ranks adaptive quotas above the other governed conditions: monitoring with sanctions reaches held-out collapse \(0.0016\), stock \(126.52\), and welfare \(6800.78\), while temporary closures reach collapse \(0.0\), stock \(124.05\), and welfare \(4719.58\). These results indicate that the first-study ranking is not confined to hand-written threshold strategies. A learned-policy family recovers adaptive quotas as the strongest medium-tier governance signal.

\subsection{Second study: governance architecture in Harvest Commons}

The second study applies the same invasion protocol used in Fishery Commons rather than relying only on a fixed-composition comparison. Precursor experiments also included none and bottom-up-only conditions, but the decision-critical evidence reported here is restricted to top-down-only versus hybrid. The fixed-composition Harvest matrix and the initial invasion pass with lighter non-LLM injectors both identified hybrid governance as the stronger architecture in this narrowed comparison. The principal Harvest evidence comes from the search-over-mutations cycle. Its Stage B pilot showed that hybrid governance remained competitive under a stronger invader: hybrid ranked first in 13 of 16 pilot cells, whereas top-down-only ranked first in the remaining 3. The main Harvest Commons evidence therefore comes from the Stage C high-power search-over-mutations matrix, which restricts attention to the decision-critical rows in the medium and hard tiers, the balanced and adversarial-heavy partner mixes, top-down-only versus hybrid governance, and pressures 0.3 and 0.5.

In that stronger high-power matrix, hybrid governance ranks first in all 8 decision cells under the Stage C failure-then-patch-health-then-welfare rule. The central inferential question is whether hybrid continues to outperform top-down-only control once the invader actively searches for more damaging children. The Stage C results show that it does, although the associated trade-off is sharper than in earlier Harvest experiments.

The aggregated Stage C view makes that trade-off readable. Patch health rises in every single decision cell, and every patch-health confidence interval remains above zero. In the hard tier with an adversarial-heavy population, the paired patch-health delta is \(+0.0157\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(0.0027\) to \(0.0264\), while the welfare delta is \(-0.0883\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(-0.1668\) to \(-0.0119\). In the hard tier with a balanced population at pressure 0.3, hybrid improves patch health by \(+0.1356\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(0.0303\) to \(0.2722\), but welfare falls by \(0.2306\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(-0.3922\) to \(-0.0643\).

Medium-tier effects are more heterogeneous, but remain directionally consistent. In the adversarial-heavy block at pressure 0.3, patch health rises by \(+0.0474\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(0.0140\) to \(0.1019\), while welfare overlaps a modest cost. In the balanced block at pressure 0.3, both patch health and welfare favor hybrid in point estimates, although the welfare interval crosses zero. The largest welfare penalty appears in the medium balanced block at pressure 0.5, where patch health still rises by \(+0.0438\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(0.0052\) to \(0.0863\), but welfare falls by \(0.2866\), with a 95\% confidence interval from \(-0.5390\) to \(-0.0702\). These results do not imply universal metric dominance or zero welfare cost for hybrid governance. The narrower conclusion is that, under a stronger search-based invader, hybrid governance preserves the commons more effectively than top-down-only control under the manuscript's ranking rule, while the welfare cost depends strongly on social composition and pressure.

These mechanism metrics explain why the failure metric is uninformative in the Harvest Commons study. Across all 8 high-power rows, the paired garden-failure delta is exactly zero, so governance separation happens before outright garden failure. Hybrid governance matters because it shifts local behavior: averaged over the full Stage C matrix, local aggression falls by \(0.0037\) and neighborhood overharvest falls by \(0.0646\) relative to top-down-only control. The stronger result is therefore behavioral and ecological rather than catastrophic, and the 8-of-8 ranking result should be read through those mechanism margins rather than as a claim of metric-by-metric domination.

A narrow learned-policy validation yields concordant evidence. Under five PPO self-play training runs on the held-out `medium\_h1` pack, hybrid raises mean patch health from \(15.5673\) under top-down-only control to \(15.8064\), lowers mean max local aggression from \(0.6\) to \(0.4\), and lowers mean neighborhood overharvest from \(9.0\) to \(7.65\). Mean welfare falls only slightly, from \(16.3757\) to \(16.2898\). Because garden failure is again saturated at zero in this medium-tier RL setting, the learned-policy check is informative only on the same local mechanism margins that matter in the invasion matrix. It should therefore be read as supporting evidence for the architecture ranking, not as a substitute for the stronger search-over-mutations result.

Taken together, the two studies provide a layered invasion result. In the controlled fishery setting, central governance signals can be ranked, and adaptive quotas emerge as the strongest overall top-down signal under invasion pressure. In the second substrate, once governance is allowed to become architectural rather than only signal-level, the narrower top-down-only versus hybrid comparison shows that hybrid produces better local ecological control under a stronger search-based invader, although the welfare effect depends on both social composition and task difficulty.
