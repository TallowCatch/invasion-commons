\section{Introduction}

Classic commons theory predicts over-extraction under open access and highlights institutional design as the key lever for sustainable outcomes \citep{gordon1954common,scott1955fishery,ostrom1990governing}. In machine learning, sequential social dilemma research and benchmark design emphasize policy-level dynamics, long horizons, and held-out evaluation rather than one-shot game payoffs \citep{leibo2017multiagent,meltingpot2021,du2023review}. Recent LLM-agent commons work has shown that cooperation can emerge in simulated societies, but robustness under distribution shift and repeated adversarial pressure remains weakly characterized \citep{piatti2024cooperate,govsim_repo}.

This work studies governance robustness in a minimal sequential fishery commons under adversarial strategy injection. The core research question is: \emph{Which governance signals stabilize cooperation under adversarial strategy injection, and does this hold across regime difficulty and injector type (mutation versus live LLM generation)?}

The key gap is a missing \emph{invasion-stability protocol} for commons governance. Prior commons simulations typically evaluate emergence in mostly fixed populations, while continuous adversarial generation pressure has been explored mainly in other domains (for example, program-level Red Queen dynamics) \citep{sakana2026drq}. Broader policy discussions around generative-agent societies also stress realism and evaluation discipline but do not provide this protocol for renewable commons governance \citep{mercer2023willowbrook}. This leaves an open problem: measuring whether governance signals remain effective when new exploitative strategies are repeatedly generated and injected.

The evaluation is designed as a matched comparison rather than a one-off demonstration. We use the same experiment budget and protocol across governance conditions, perform selection in train regimes, and evaluate robustness in held-out regimes. Outcomes are reported with uncertainty intervals from run-level summaries.

The cycle contribution is an evidence package for invasion-stability rather than a new environment feature. Concretely, we provide a matched governance matrix over two benchmark tiers (easy\_v1 and medium\_v1) and two injector types (mutation and live llm\_json), confidence-interval reporting for collapse reduction and supporting robustness metrics, a composite governance ranking that aggregates collapse, stock, and survival effects, and explicit gate logic that determines whether claims are supported or invalidated by regime saturation.

Hard-tier results are treated as appendix stress evidence only. Primary claims are based on medium-tier matched runs, where governance separation is measurable and non-saturated.
